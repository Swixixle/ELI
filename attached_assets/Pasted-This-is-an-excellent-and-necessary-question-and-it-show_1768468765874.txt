This is an excellent and necessary question — and it shows you’re now thinking like someone building a credible system, not just a usable one.

I’m going to answer it in a way that is blunt, grounding, and actionable.

⸻

First: name the fear correctly

What you’re really asking is not:

“Why would they believe the numbers?”

It’s:

“Why would anyone trust that this engine isn’t just vibes dressed up as rigor?”

That is the right fear to have.

And the answer is not “because it’s smart,” or “because Canon,” or “because AI.”

Trust does not come from the formula.
Trust comes from how the formula exposes its limits and provenance.

⸻

The key principle (this is non-negotiable)

You do not earn trust by claiming correctness.
You earn trust by making falsification and inspection possible.

Serious users don’t ask:
	•	“Is this right?”

They ask:
	•	“Could I catch you being wrong?”

Your system must make that possible.

⸻

Why “sample numbers” are the wrong framing (important reframe)

If you try to convince people that:
	•	You picked the right thresholds
	•	You tuned the right weights
	•	You found the correct formula

You will lose credibility.

Because no one believes that in governance, law, finance, or ethics.

Instead, the trust claim must be:

“We are not claiming the perfect formula.
We are claiming a transparent, bounded, defensible process.”

That is a winnable claim.

⸻

What people actually trust in systems like this

In high-stakes domains, trust comes from four signals, not one.

I’ll walk through each and show you how to surface it.

⸻

1. Process visibility (not raw math)

People don’t need to see equations.
They need to see decision stages.

What they want to know:
	•	What was checked?
	•	In what order?
	•	Under what constraints?
	•	What could have changed the outcome?

How you show this

In Audit Mode only, you expose:
	•	Evaluation stages (gatekeeper → sufficiency → limits → determination)
	•	Which stage blocked or passed
	•	What inputs were considered admissible
	•	What inputs were excluded

This tells them:

“Even if I disagree, I understand how you got there.”

That’s trust.

⸻

2. Counterfactual sensitivity (“What would change this?”)

This is huge.

People trust systems that can say:

“If X were different, the answer would change.”

That proves the engine is responsive, not dogmatic.

How you show this

For any determination, include:
	•	“This conclusion would change if:”
	•	One additional document were added
	•	Independent verification were present
	•	Temporal coverage improved
	•	Ambiguity dropped below threshold

You are showing:
	•	The model is conditional
	•	The numbers are not arbitrary
	•	The user can move the needle

⸻

3. Explicit uncertainty bands (this is critical)

Never present outputs as binary truth.

Instead, present confidence classes, not confidence scores.

Example:
	•	“Procedurally sufficient”
	•	“Borderline / insufficient”
	•	“Insufficient”
	•	“Not evaluable”

These are categories, not probabilities.

Why this matters:
	•	It avoids false precision
	•	It aligns with legal and audit reasoning
	•	It signals epistemic humility

People trust systems that say:

“Here is the bucket, not a fake decimal.”

⸻

4. Audit replayability

This is where belief becomes verifiable.

Your strongest trust claim is:

“Given the same inputs and the same rules, this engine will always reach the same determination.”

That’s not about being right.
That’s about being consistent.

How you show this
	•	Versioned Canon
	•	Logged evaluation time
	•	Frozen admissible inputs
	•	Re-runnable audit trail

Anyone can replay the case and see:
	•	No hidden tuning
	•	No moving goalposts
	•	No post-hoc adjustment

That’s how regulators learn to trust systems.

⸻

The subtle but crucial distinction you must make explicit

You must never claim:

“The engine determines the truth.”

You should claim:

“The engine determines what can be responsibly concluded given the record.”

That difference is everything.

It reframes numbers from:
	•	“Correctness claims” → ❌
to
	•	“Decision eligibility claims” → ✅

⸻

How to answer the skeptical question directly (you will get this)

Someone will ask:

“Why should I believe your thresholds are right?”

Your answer should be:

“You don’t have to believe they’re right.
You only have to see that they are:
	•	Explicit
	•	Consistent
	•	Reviewable
	•	Conservative under uncertainty
	•	And open to revision”

That is how serious systems defend themselves.

⸻

How to SHOW this in the product (not marketing)

You surface trust through affordances, not explanations:
	•	“Show what would change this”
	•	“View audit trail”
	•	“Replay evaluation”
	•	“Canon v4.0 applied”
	•	“Decision blocked at sufficiency stage”

These are trust signals embedded in use.

⸻

The grounding truth (read this carefully)

No one will trust you because you “figured out the formula.”

They will trust you because:
	•	You didn’t hide the formula
	•	You didn’t overclaim it
	•	You showed where it breaks
	•	You allowed disagreement without collapse

That’s not weakness.
That’s how trust actually forms in governance.

It comes from the system being honest about what the math can and cannot justify.
