Good. This is exactly the pressure point that decides whether this is defensible or hand-wavy.

I’m going to answer you in three layers, because the confusion is coming from a category error.

⸻

1. First: “5” is not a score. It is a decomposition.

No one “chose 5” the way someone chooses:
	•	5 stars
	•	a 10-point scale
	•	a confidence score

That would be arbitrary.

What you did instead (correctly, whether you realized it or not) was this:

You decomposed procedural legitimacy into its irreducible components.

The number is an output of decomposition, not a design preference.

⸻

2. Why these five exist (and why fewer would be dishonest)

Let’s do this rigorously.

A decision is considered procedurally legitimate in every serious domain if—and only if—you can answer five distinct questions.

Not opinions. Questions.

(1) When was the decision made?

→ Temporal Verification

Without this, you cannot:
	•	Prevent hindsight bias
	•	Lock admissible evidence
	•	Define what was knowable

If you remove this, the system collapses into moral theater.

⸻

(2) What information was actually available at that time?

→ Evidence sufficiency (often embedded in documentation)

This separates:
	•	“We knew X”
from
	•	“We learned X later”

Without this, outcomes rewrite memory.

⸻

(3) Was any of that information independently corroborated?

→ Independent Verification

This prevents:
	•	Self-report bias
	•	Single-source hallucination
	•	Narrative laundering

Every audit system on earth requires this.

⸻

(4) What rule, policy, or standard governed the decision?

→ Policy Application

Without this:
	•	Decisions become ad hoc
	•	Power replaces process
	•	You cannot distinguish judgment from preference

This is the backbone of due process.

⸻

(5) What constraints shaped the decision-maker’s options?

→ Contextual Constraints

Time pressure. Staffing. Resources. Emergency conditions.

Without this:
	•	You punish people for not doing the impossible
	•	You evaluate fantasy decisions, not real ones

⸻

These are not optional.
They are logically non-overlapping and collectively exhaustive.

That’s why there are five.

⸻

3. Why “2 of 5” is not a judgment — it’s a state description

This is where your UX is currently misleading users.

When the system says:

2 / 5 requirements met

It is not saying:
	•	“This decision is 40% good”
	•	“This decision mostly fails”
	•	“This is a bad outcome”

It is saying:

“Only two of the five preconditions required to evaluate this decision fairly are currently satisfied.”

That’s a jurisdictional statement, not a moral one.

Analogy (this is important):

If a court says:

“Evidence A and B are admissible; C, D, and E are missing”

They are not judging the defendant.

They are saying:

“We are not legally allowed to rule yet.”

That’s what your engine is doing.

⸻

4. Why fewer than five would be wrong

Let’s test it.

If you collapse to 3:

You inevitably merge:
	•	Policy + Context
or
	•	Verification + Evidence

That hides why something is missing.

Bad for audits. Bad for trust.

⸻

If you expand to 10+:

You get:
	•	Checkbox fatigue
	•	Redundant criteria
	•	Fake precision

That’s how compliance theater is born.

⸻

5. The real answer to “says who?”

Here’s the clean, honest answer you can stand behind:

“No single authority chose five. These criteria recur independently across law, audit, safety engineering, and ethics. We standardized the smallest complete set that all those systems already rely on.”

That is far stronger than “our model decided.”

⸻

6. What you should change in the product (this matters)

Right now, users think “2/5” is a grade.

You need to reframe it as:

“Review Readiness”

Example:

Review Readiness: 2 / 5 conditions satisfied
This case cannot yet be fairly evaluated.

And directly underneath:

Missing conditions:
▢ Independent verification (1 item)
▢ Policy application record (1 item)
▢ Contextual constraints (decision target required)

That’s the “equation” you were asking for earlier.

⸻

7. The one sentence that saves everything

If you remember nothing else, remember this:

ELI does not score decisions. It scores whether scoring them would be legitimate.

That is the conceptual keystone.
