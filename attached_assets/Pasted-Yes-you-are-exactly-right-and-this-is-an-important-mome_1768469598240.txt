Why timestamps and documents are not “extra features”

In a governance / audit system, time is not metadata.
Time is evidence.

Any serious reviewer is silently asking:
	•	When did we know this?
	•	What did we have at that moment?
	•	What changed afterward?
	•	What was unavailable at decision time?

If the system does not visibly anchor itself in time and documents, it feels like:
	•	A black box
	•	A vibes engine
	•	A post-hoc rationalizer

So yes — timestamps and document visibility are table stakes, not polish.

⸻

What the system should be able to show (concretely)

In Advisor Mode (default, human):

A simple timeline panel

Not logs. Not raw audit trails. A story of the case.

Example:

Case timeline
	•	Jan 12, 09:14 — Policy document uploaded
	•	Jan 12, 09:16 — Case summary added
	•	Jan 12, 09:18 — Evidence log uploaded
	•	Jan 12, 09:20 — Review requested

That alone answers half of a user’s confusion.

⸻

A “Documents considered” section

This is crucial for trust.

Example:

Documents used in this assessment
	•	Case Summary (v1.0)
	•	Policy Document (v1.0)
	•	Evidence Log (v1.0)

Optional link:
	•	▸ View document list
	•	▸ View audit reasoning (advanced)

This tells the user:

“The engine is not hallucinating. It is looking here.”

⸻

Why the CFO calculator matters (and why you’re right to compare)

The CFO calculator worked because it did three things well:
	1.	Visible inputs
Users could see what numbers went in.
	2.	Deterministic outputs
Same inputs → same result.
	3.	Counterfactual play
“What if I change this?”

That’s exactly the trust pattern you want here — just applied to governance instead of finance.

So yes: they are philosophically the same product, not competitors.

⸻

How they fit together (this is the unifying frame)

Think of it this way:
	•	The CFO calculator answers:
“Given these numbers, what follows financially?”
	•	The ELI governance engine answers:
“Given these documents at this time, what follows procedurally?”

Both are:
	•	Input-bound
	•	Time-bound
	•	Replayable
	•	Conservative under uncertainty

That’s why they belong together.

⸻

What “used together” actually means (not hand-wavy)

Scenario example

A case involves a financial decision.

The system can show:

Governance view
	•	Documentation sufficiency
	•	Policy admissibility
	•	Decision limits
	•	Audit defensibility

Financial view (CFO calculator)
	•	Financial exposure
	•	Risk ranges
	•	Sensitivity to assumptions
	•	Worst/best-case bounds

Together, they answer the real executive question:

“Is this decision both procedurally defensible and financially sane given what we knew at the time?”

That is powerful — and rare.
