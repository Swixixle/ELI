Yes—this is possible, and the direction you’re describing is the right product move: the app opens as a guided expert, not a toolkit.

A clean way to think about it:

On first load, the product “performs itself.”
It teaches the buyer how to interrogate it, proves it’s honest, then gets out of the way.

Below is what to implement (and what to tell your agent/architect). This keeps UI minimal, uses visuals only when they demonstrate the mechanism, and makes the system self-explanatory.

⸻

What you want, precisely

Core experience
	1.	App opens → one prominent box:
“Ask how to use me.”
(also clickable as a one-tap prompt)
	2.	User asks “how do I use this?” → the ELI Expert runs a scripted demo:
	•	What ELI is (conceptually)
	•	What it can/can’t do (epistemic boundaries)
	•	How it proves claims (citations + calc proof)
	•	A quick “try this” set of prompts (3–5)
	3.	Then it transitions into one interface:
	•	A single chat input
	•	Optional mode toggle hidden behind a simple “Audience” dropdown (Buyer / CFO / Engineer) if needed
	•	Visuals appear only when the model chooses “Show how this works”

Minimalism requirement

No dashboards. No gadgets. No sprawling controls.
All “advanced controls” should be collapsed and only appear when a user asks.

⸻

The “presentation” should not be a slideshow

It should be a guided, interactive walkthrough inside the chat.

Format: 4 panels max, inside the conversation
	•	Panel 1: What this is
	•	Panel 2: What it refuses
	•	Panel 3: How it proves numbers
	•	Panel 4: Ask me anything (with examples)

Each panel can use a small visual card (not a full page) with one diagram/table max.

⸻

The essential visual demonstrations (only these)
	1.	Evidence Badges demo

	•	One example answer with [CANON] and [PUBLIC SOURCE]
	•	A tooltip: “Every claim must have a source.”

	2.	Calc proof demo

	•	A small numeric example with “Show Math”
	•	Demonstrate [SEALED PARAMETER] as “IP-protected factor”
	•	The point: “We prove arithmetic without exposing internals.”

	3.	Parrot Box / refusal demo

	•	A question that triggers refusal
	•	The point: “We don’t pretend to know what cannot be known.”

That’s it. Everything else is optional.

⸻

“Importing you into the program” (what’s realistic)

You can absolutely “import you” in the relevant sense:
	•	Your canon
	•	Your positioning language
	•	Your constraints and tone
	•	Your preferred explanations (PhD-level clarity)

But it should be framed as:

“The assistant is trained on your canonical materials and governed by strict admissibility rules.”

Not as literal identity import. The effect can be what you want: a credible proxy.

⸻

The PhD-level requirement (how to implement cleanly)

Do this with a single toggle in the response contract, not UI clutter:
	•	Default: Executive clarity
	•	If user types “deep dive” or selects “PhD mode”:
	•	More formal structure
	•	Definitions, assumptions, proofs
	•	Implementation detail level increased
	•	Still citation-bound

Important: PhD mode must still refuse when sources don’t support the claim.
