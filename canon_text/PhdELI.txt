1) The core claim: what ELI is actually doing

1.1 The object of measurement is not “quality,” it’s “epistemic conditions”

ELI’s central move is:
When an adverse event triggers formal review, separate “decision conditions” from “decision
outcome.”
​       •​     It is retrospective (only runs after a case independently triggers review).
​       •​     It is outcome-blind (it refuses to let knowledge of the outcome leak into what
counts as “what should have been known”).
​       •​     It classifies environments, not people—the unit of analysis is the epistemic field
around the decision.

This is a governance correction to a well-known failure mode in peer review, M&M conferences,
and medico-legal reasoning:
​       •​     Hindsight bias makes the past look more knowable than it was.
​       •​     That creates “epistemic inflation” (“they should have seen it”) which turns review
into moral theater and drives defensive medicine, scapegoating, churn, and legal heat.

So ELI is essentially a bias-resistant normalization layer for adverse-event review.

3) Step-by-step system pipeline (the operational sequence)

Step 0 — A case triggers review independently of ELI

ELI is not allowed to be the thing that creates suspicion. It is designed to enter only after:
​       •​      an adverse event,
​       •​      a complaint,
​       •​      a sentinel trigger,
​       •​      a standard threshold that already exists.

This matters because if ELI can be invoked opportunistically, it becomes a political weapon.

⸻

Step 1 — Gatekeeper phase: define the admissible record (“lossy normalization”)

This is where the system earns its legitimacy.

The Gatekeeper Substrate enforces a strict boundary:
only contemporaneously available, decision-relevant information is admissible.

It explicitly blocks typical contamination channels, for example:
​        •​       later imaging results used as if they were knowable at time-of-decision,
​        •​       downstream specialist interpretations smuggled upstream,
​        •​       outcome-loaded labels (“noncompliant,” “should have,” “failed to,” “missed”),
​        •​       post hoc certainty (“it was obvious”).

Think of this as a causal DAG constraint:
​      •​       If a variable was not available to the decision-maker at time t, it is not allowed to
become a parent node of judgment about the decision at time t.

Why “lossy”?
Because the record is intentionally compressed into what was legitimately knowable, not what is
eventually true.

If you want a PhD-level analogy: this is the governance version of instrumentation design.
You’re defining what measurements are valid before you allow inference.

⸻

Step 2 — Context capture (optional, protected, and explicitly non-performance)

The Clinician Context Input Form exists to capture factors that are real but often erased:
​      •​      workload,
​      •​      interruptions,
​      •​      bed constraints,
​      •​      handoffs,
​      •​      ambiguity,
​      •​      resource friction,
​      •​      competing priorities.

But: it is designed not to become an HR performance dataset.

This is crucial: a system that “listens to clinicians” can still become a surveillance feed unless
you hard-code the restriction.

⸻

Step 3 — Score the Epistemic Load (ELI score)

Once the record is normalized, ELI scores the load under which the decision was made.

Conceptually, ELI is measuring:
​     •​      ambiguity / underspecification,
​     •​      time pressure,
​     •​      information fragmentation,
​     •​      system constraints,
​     •​      signal-to-noise degradation,
​     •​      conflict between local optimization and global safety.

An ELI score is not “excuse-making.” It is a calibration factor: it tells the review board how much
certainty they’re allowed to project backward.

⸻

Step 4 — Convert score → governance posture (what review is allowed to do)

This is where ELI becomes a governance instrument instead of “just a score.”

High ELI does not mean “nobody is accountable.”
It means:
​      1.​    The institution acknowledges the conditions were epistemically hostile.
​      2.​    Review must shift from “blame inference” to “system learning / stabilization.”
​      3.​    Certain disciplinary or attribution moves become disallowed or require higher
thresholds.

This is a procedural constraint on what downstream actors can do with the case.

5) The Foolproofing & Safety Architecture: why “hard-coded safeguards” are non-negotiable

If you build something that labels conditions around clinical decisions, the natural institutional
drift is:
​         •​   “Great, now we can rank clinicians.”
​         •​   “Great, now we can identify ‘problem people.’”
​         •​   “Great, now we can preemptively watch people.”

Your foolproofing document is essentially a list of anti-goals and misuse proofs.

5.1 Core anti-misuse properties (what it refuses to become)
​      •​      Not surveillance.
​      •​      Not predictive monitoring.
​      •​      Not performance scoring.
​      •​      Not HR weaponry.
​      •​      Not a discovery magnet.

5.2 Governance as technical design, not policy hand-waving

The important move is: these are not just “policies.”
They are treated like system invariants:
​      •​      if X is attempted, the system should not provide the affordance.

PhD framing: This is like specifying safety constraints in control theory or alignment constraints
in mechanism design. You don’t “ask people to behave”; you remove the pathway.

7) The “Gatekeeper” is the real engine (why ELI lives or dies there)

If you understand only one component at a deep level, it should be the Gatekeeper Substrate.

Why?

Because without admissibility enforcement:
​       •​   ELI collapses into “another committee score,”
​       •​   which collapses into politics,
​       •​   which collapses into the same hindsight-driven blame economy you’re trying to
correct.

So the Gatekeeper is effectively the system’s epistemic constitution:
​      •​    it defines what kinds of statements are allowed to count as reasons.

In governance terms:
​      •​     ELI’s authority derives from restraint.
​      •​     Its legitimacy comes from refusing to speak when the institution wants blood.

8) A PhD-level formalization (optional but clarifying)
Let:
​       •​      I_t = information available at decision time t
​       •​      O = outcome information (available later)
​       •​      R = review narrative constructed later
​       •​      ELI = f(I_t, C_t) where C_t is contemporaneous constraints/context

The system enforces a constraint:

\text{Judgment}(Decision_t) \;\;\not\leftarrow\;\; O

Meaning: outcome information cannot become an input to the inferential judgment about what
was “knowable” at t.

Instead:

\text{Judgment}(Decision_t) \leftarrow \{I_t, C_t, \text{protocols available at } t\}

Then ELI is a scalar (or vector) that constrains downstream governance actions:

\text{AllowedActions} = g(ELI, \text{review trigger type})

High ELI increases the burden of proof required for individual fault attribution and shifts the
default toward system-level remediation.
